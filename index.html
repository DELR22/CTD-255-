Section 01

What This Course Is About

CTD 255 guides students through the integrated digital workflow of contemporary interior design practice, using a design-process framework rather than a software-by-software checklist. Students follow instructor-produced video tutorials to complete a single interior design case study, tracing the design process from AI-assisted concept generation through spatial planning, technical documentation, and final presentation.

AutoCAD is introduced as a tool for schematic block planning and area calculation, while Autodesk Revit serves as the primary environment for all interior modeling and technical documentation. Twinmotion is used to develop real-time 3D visualizations directly linked to the Revit model, and Adobe Photoshop, combined with AI image-refining tools, is used for post-production rendering and final presentation graphics.

One Case Study, One Continuous Workflow

Every tool in this course is introduced in service of the same project, carried from a vague design concept all the way to a finished presentation board. There is no tool taught in isolation—each one picks up exactly where the last one left off.

The case study itself is a six-story industrial building retrofitted into a student residence—a real adaptive-reuse problem with fixed structural constraints.

Section 02

The Workflow — Four Stages

Every assignment belongs to one of four sequential design stages. Each stage hands its output directly to the next—a concept becomes a block plan, a block plan becomes a model, a model becomes a render.

Stage 01

Design Concept

Human-centered design principles frame AI-assisted concept generation, curated into a written design narrative that defines spatial atmosphere, material palette, and user experience.

AI Concept Generation

Stage 02

Schematic Design

The concept becomes a schematic block plan—spatial zones, circulation, and program components organized and area-calculated against the design brief.

AutoCAD

Stage 03

Construction Documents

The block plan becomes a complete BIM model, then a coordinated set of dimensioned plans, elevations, sections, and a reflected ceiling plan.

Autodesk Revit

Stage 04

Project Delivery

The Revit model becomes a real-time visualization package, then a post-produced, professionally formatted presentation board.

Section 03

Pedagogical Approach & Philosophy

This is a virtual, primarily asynchronous course built for autonomous learners. Three principles shape how it runs.

Principle 01

Judgment Over Memorization

Assessment rewards methodological understanding, purposeful tool integration, and design coherence—not software proficiency or instruction-following alone. Knowing which tool to reach for, and why, matters more than knowing every menu in it.

Principle 02

Autonomy Is Expected and Respected

Most work happens independently through instructor-produced video tutorials completed at your own pace, in sequence. Twice-weekly synchronous sessions are project reviews, not lectures—attendance is not mandatory, but missing a session means missing feedback before the next deadline.

Principle 03

Revision Is Normal, Not Remedial

Complete, on-time submissions are eligible for resubmission with a written Revision Statement explaining what changed and why. The Collaborative Process Board tracks growth across all four stages rather than penalizing a single rough check-in.

Section 04

Student Learning Outcomes

By the end of the course, you will be able to do the following, each tied to specific CIDA and NASAD accreditation standards.

  • SLO 01 BIM-Based Technical Documentation — Demonstrate technical proficiency in Autodesk Revit by producing a complete and accurately dimensioned set of interior construction documents, including floor plans, reflected ceiling plans, elevations, and section cuts.
  • SLO 02 Rendering & Spatial Visualization — Employ rendering engines, methods, and tools to generate 3D images that intentionally communicate materiality, light quality, and spatial atmosphere as deliberate design communication decisions.
  • SLO 03 Critical Technology Selection & Integration — Demonstrate critical judgment in selecting and integrating digital tools at appropriate phases of the design process, articulating each tool's communicative purpose, representational advantages, and limitations.
  • SLO 04 Composite Presentation Graphics — Apply digital representation tools and methods to synthesize outputs into a coherent, persuasive visual narrative that supports a documented design workflow.
  • SLO 05 Iterative, Technology-Mediated Refinement — Deploy digital representation tools iteratively as instruments of design inquiry, using outputs from each application to critically evaluate and refine spatial organization, materials, and lighting across successive iterations.
